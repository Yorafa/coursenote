Group Definition

Binary operation: a function map G×G→GG\times G \to GG×G→G, denote such function by ⋅\cdot⋅ then we can write ⋅(x,y)\cdot(x,y)⋅(x,y) or x⋅yx\cdot yx⋅y to present the operation acting on x,yx,yx,y.

  • ∀x,y,z∈G,(x⋅y)⋅z=x⋅(y⋅z)  ⟺  ⋅\forall x,y,z \in G, (x\cdot y)\cdot z = x\cdot (y\cdot z) \iff \cdot∀x,y,z∈G,(x⋅y)⋅z=x⋅(y⋅z)⟺⋅ is associative
  • ∀a,b∈G,a⋅b=b⋅a  ⟺  ⋅\forall a,b \in G, a\cdot b = b\cdot a \iff \cdot∀a,b∈G,a⋅b=b⋅a⟺⋅ is commutative

Some e.g.

  1. in Z,+:Z×Z→Z\mathbb{Z}, +:\mathbb{Z}\times \mathbb{Z}\to \mathbb{Z}Z,+:Z×Z→Z
  2. in R,⋅:R×R→R\mathbb{R}, \cdot: \mathbb{R}\times \mathbb{R} \to \mathbb{R}R,⋅:R×R→R

Group​

Group is triple (G,e,⋅)(G, e, \cdot)(G,e,⋅), ⋅:G×G→G\cdot: G\times G \to G⋅:G×G→G is binary operation, e∈Ge\in Ge∈G, satisfied:

  1. ∀x,y,z∈G,(x⋅y)⋅z=x⋅(y⋅z)\forall x,y,z \in G, (x\cdot y)\cdot z = x\cdot (y\cdot z) ∀x,y,z∈G,(x⋅y)⋅z=x⋅(y⋅z) (associativity)
  2. ∀a∈G,a⋅e=e⋅a=a\forall a \in G, a\cdot e=e\cdot a = a∀a∈G,a⋅e=e⋅a=a, e∈Ge\in Ge∈G is the identity of G (identity)
  3. ∀a∈G,∃b∈G,a⋅b=b⋅a=e\forall a\in G, \exists b\in G, a\cdot b = b\cdot a = e∀a∈G,∃b∈G,a⋅b=b⋅a=e, b is the inverse of aaa
  4. ∀x,y,x∈G∧y∈G  ⟹  x⋅y∈G∧y⋅x∈G\forall x,y, x \in G \land y \in G \implies x\cdot y \in G \land y \cdot x \in G∀x,y,x∈G∧y∈G⟹x⋅y∈G∧y⋅x∈G (closure)

e.g. ({0},0,⋅)(\{0\}, 0, \cdot)({0},0,⋅) , (R,0,+)(\mathbb{R}, 0, +)(R,0,+), (R\{0},0,⋅)(\mathbb{R}\backslash\{0\}, 0, \cdot)(R\{0},0,⋅), ({e},e,⋅),e⋅e=e(\{e\}, e, \cdot), e\cdot e = e({e},e,⋅),e⋅e=e

We may find some element are special where we can use them to generate the group by use group operation start from identity, such element is called generators of the group.

Group Theorem​

  1. Group's identity and inverse is unique (uniqueness of identity and inverse)
  2. ∀a,b,c∈G,a⋅b=b⋅c  ⟹  a=c\forall a,b,c \in G, a\cdot b = b\cdot c\implies a= c∀a,b,c∈G,a⋅b=b⋅c⟹a=c and ∀a,b,c∈G,b⋅a=c⋅b  ⟹  a=c\forall a,b,c \in G, b\cdot a = c\cdot b\implies a= c∀a,b,c∈G,b⋅a=c⋅b⟹a=c (cancellation law)
  3. ∀a,b∈G,(a⋅b)−1=b−1⋅a−1\forall a,b \in G, (a\cdot b)^{-1} = b^{-1}\cdot a^{-1}∀a,b∈G,(a⋅b)−1=b−1⋅a−1 (Socks-Shoes Property)

Subgroup Test​

Let GGG be a group:

  • If H⊆GH\sube GH⊆G is a nonempty subset, and ∀a,b∈H  ⟹  a⋅b−1∈H\forall a,b \in H \implies a\cdot b^{-1} \in H∀a,b∈H⟹a⋅b−1∈H, then HHH is a subgroup of GGG. (One-step test)
  • If HHH is nonempty subset of GGG, (a,b∈H  ⟹  ab∈H)∧(a−1∈H)(a,b\in H \implies ab\in H) \land (a^{-1}\in H)(a,b∈H⟹ab∈H)∧(a−1∈H), then HHH is a subgroup of GGG (two-step test)
  • If HHH is nonempty finite subset of GGG and HHH is closed under the operation of GGG, then HHH is a subgroup of GGG. (finite subgroup test)
  • The center of GGG is a subgroup of GGG
  • The centralizer of GGG is a subgroup of GGG
  • g∈Gg\in Gg∈G, the cyclic group ⟨g⟩\langle g\rangle⟨g⟩ is a subgroup of GGG.
